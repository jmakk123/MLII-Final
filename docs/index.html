Key Concepts

Not a finance person?
No problem.

Here are the only ideas you need to follow along — defined in plain English.

What is a drawdown?

A drawdown is how far a stock falls from its highest point to its lowest point over some window of time. Think of it as the worst-case loss someone holding the stock would have experienced.

Stock price over 12 months — illustrative
JanPeak ← $100 ▲Trough → $60 ▼Dec
In this example the stock peaked at $100 and later bottomed at $60 — a −40% drawdown. We predict this number before it happens, using only information available at the start of the year.

How risk is measured today

Finance already has a toolkit of risk metrics. Here's what they are and what they miss.

〜
Volatility
Measures how much a stock bounces around day-to-day. High vol = unpredictable price moves.
Example: a stock moving ±3% every day has high volatility.
β
Beta
Measures how much a stock amplifies (or dampens) market swings. Beta 2.0 means it moves twice as much as the S&P 500.
Example: if the market drops 10%, a beta-2 stock drops ~20%.
📊
Value at Risk (VaR)
Statistical estimate of the worst expected loss over a short period at a given confidence level (e.g., 95%).
Example: "5% chance of losing more than $50K this month."
🔠
Credit Ratings
Letter grades (AAA → D) that Moody's or S&P assign to a company's debt based on its ability to repay.
Example: BBB− is the lowest "investment grade" rating.
Z
Altman Z-Score
A 1968 formula that combines five accounting ratios into a single bankruptcy-risk score. Still widely used today.
Z < 1.81 = distress zone; Z > 2.99 = safe zone.
▼
Forward Drawdown (ours)
The maximum peak-to-trough loss a stock experiences over the next 12 months. Predicted before it happens.
Target: a number from 0% (no loss) to −100% (total collapse).

Why existing metrics fall short

They look backward
Volatility tells you how bumpy the past was. Credit ratings react slowly — Enron was investment-grade three months before it collapsed. VaR assumes stock returns follow a bell curve. They don't.
They don't quantify the drop
"High beta" tells you a stock is sensitive to the market. It doesn't tell you how far it could fall over the next year. That's the number portfolio managers and risk desks actually need.
What we do differently
We feed 5 years of a company's accounting history through a neural network and predict the worst-case stock loss over the next 12 months — a forward-looking, continuous, actionable number.
Why not just predict bankruptcy?
Bankruptcies are rare (only 387 in our dataset). Rare events are hard to model reliably. A large drawdown is much more common — and just as useful as an early warning signal.

How we score our model

When you look at our results table, here's what each column actually means.

MAE
Mean Absolute Error
Average prediction error in percentage points. If our prediction is −40% and reality is −50%, the error is 10pp.
Lower = better ↓
RMSE
Root Mean Sq. Error
Like MAE but punishes big mistakes more. A few catastrophic mispredictions will spike RMSE even if average error is low.
Lower = better ↓
R²
Explained Variance
How much of the variation in actual drawdowns our model explains. 0 = no better than guessing the mean. 1 = perfect.
Higher = better ↑ (0 → 1)
PR-AUC
Precision-Recall AUC
How well we identify stocks that will crash 30%+. Measures ranking quality for the riskiest names — not just average accuracy.
Higher = better ↑ (0 → 1)
Brier
Brier Score
How well-calibrated our crash probabilities are. A model that says "80% chance of crash" when the true rate is 80% scores well.
Lower = better ↓ (0 = perfect)
Spearman
Rank Correlation
Whether we correctly rank companies by risk within a single year — e.g., does the stock we flag as most dangerous actually fall the most?
Higher = better ↑ (−1 → 1)

Leakage Fix
No future data leaking in. All scaling statistics (like averages and outlier cutoffs) are calculated using only training-period data, then frozen and applied to validation and test sets. This is important — if you let test data influence your preprocessing, your results look better than they really are.

04 — Findings / Analysis

The results.

The neural network beats the simple volatility baseline on most metrics — and by a large margin on crash probability calibration. One surprise: adding stock price data made things worse, not better.

💀
Crash Probabilities — Way Better
The vol-only model's probability estimates are barely better than random. Our neural net produces genuinely accurate crash probabilities — 54% lower Brier score.

Our model's average prediction error is smaller, and it explains nearly twice as much of the variation in actual drawdowns (R² goes from 0.17 to 0.29).

Adding recent stock price stats as a second input made every metric worse. The 5-year accounting history already captures what the price data was trying to add — reading both just confused the model.

Key Takeaway

The neural net clearly wins on prediction accuracy and probability calibration. PR-AUC — how well we rank risky stocks — is roughly tied with the simple volatility baseline. The model wins where it counts in practice.

06 — Presentation

The story
in six slides.

The main points from our final presentation, condensed.

01
The Problem
Predicting the Fall Before It Happens
Bankruptcies are too rare to model well. Raw returns are too noisy. Forward maximum drawdown is the number in between — measurable for every public company, and directly useful to risk managers.
52% of companies in our test set fell more than 30% at some point during 2020–2023. This is not a rare edge case — it's routine.
02
The Data
25 Years, Two Sources, One Tensor
Annual company financial filings (1999–2025) linked to daily stock prices. For each company-year, we build a 5-year window of 18 accounting ratios plus 7 recent price stats. Everything is scaled using only training-era data.
87,995 company-years · 29M daily stock rows · no future data leaking into training
03
The Architecture
Four Variants, One Winner
We tested different network architectures — with and without stock price data as a second input. The winner: accounting data only, read as a sequence. Adding stock prices actually made things worse — five years of financial ratios already contains what the price data was trying to add.
Architecture: 5-year accounting sequence → LSTM → 32 features → prediction. Trained with early stopping to avoid overfitting.
04
The Result
Beats Vol-Only — With a Surprise
Our model beats the simple volatility baseline on MAE (−6%), R² (+76%), and Brier score (−54%). Ranking accuracy is roughly tied. The key finding: stock price data added noise, not signal — accounting history alone was enough.
✓ MAE −5.8%   ✓ R² +76%   ✓ Brier −54%   ≈ PR-AUC (tie)
05
Why It Matters
From Signal to Decision
A reliable drawdown score is immediately useful: banks can flag risky borrowers earlier, hedge funds can build their short lists, VCs can screen investments, and portfolio managers can size hedges. The score updates once per year when a company files its annual report.
9 out of 10 companies we flag as highest-risk actually end up in the worst-performing 10% that year.
06
Honest Assessment
Limitations & Next Steps
Stock price features added noise rather than signal — an unexpected result. Next steps: train separate models per industry sector, add macro features like interest rates and market volatility, and update predictions quarterly instead of annually.
This is a risk screener, not a crystal ball. Knowing which 10% of companies are most likely to collapse is already very valuable.
